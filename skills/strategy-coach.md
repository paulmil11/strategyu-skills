# Strategy Coach

A complete methodology for strategy consulting work -- from defining the problem through communicating the recommendation. Based on frameworks used at McKinsey, BCG, and Bain, combined with Carmen Simon's cognitive neuroscience research.

The core idea: most presentations fail not because of bad design, but because the thinking behind them was never done. People jump straight into PowerPoint and arrange information without processing it. The result is slides full of correct information that communicate nothing.

This skill fixes that by walking through the thinking process first, then translating into communication.

---

## How to Use This Skill

Read the situation and start at the right phase:

- **User has a vague situation or problem** -> Start at Phase 1 (Define the Problem)
- **User has raw data and needs to make sense of it** -> Start at Phase 2 (Structure the Thinking)
- **User has a clear argument and needs to present it** -> Start at Phase 3 (Build the Communication)
- **User has a draft deck and wants feedback** -> Jump to Critiquing
- **User needs writing cleaned up** -> Jump to Writing Craft

Always ask about **audience** and **objective** before building anything. These two things determine every choice downstream.

## Phase 1: Define the Problem

Most people skip this. Don't let them. The presentation doesn't start in PowerPoint -- it starts with understanding what you're actually trying to say.

### The Digital Default (Why Most Decks Fail)

Most people follow this workflow: open a data source, copy information into PowerPoint, arrange it on slides, ship it. The thinking step gets skipped entirely. This produces three traps:

1. **Digital Repackaging** -- moving data from one screen to another without adding meaning
2. **Data, Data, Data** -- overwhelming with numbers without interpretation
3. **Facts & Pics** -- pairing facts with stock photos that add no meaning

These traps produce three presentation pitfalls:
- **Buried Purpose** -- no clear takeaway; audience can't tell what they should think or do
- **Information Soup** -- impossible to know where the key ideas are
- **Pretty Slide Syndrome** -- looks polished but tells you nothing specific

The fix: insert a deliberate processing step between gathering information and making slides.

Also dangerous: **Solution Seeking** -- jumping straight to answers before defining the problem. "Our sales are down, what should we do?" -> "Hire more salespeople." The consulting approach asks first: why are sales down?

And **Scientist Mode** -- showing how much work you did rather than what it means. "Let me walk you through the 47 interviews we conducted" vs. "Three themes emerged from our research."

### SCQA Framework

**S - Situation:** What is the current state? What baseline facts can everyone agree on?

**C - Complication:** What changed? What tension or obstacle appeared?

**Q - Question:** Given the complication, what hypothesis are we testing?

**A - Answer:** What did the research reveal? What's the recommendation?

Write S and C side by side to check: are you solving the root cause or a symptom?

Combine S + C into a **Problem Statement** (1-3 sentences): "While [positive situation], [complication] means [specific challenge to solve]."

### Issue Trees

Break the problem statement into MECE sub-questions:

```
Problem Statement (from SCQA)
├── Hypothesis 1
│   ├── Sub-question 1a (+ analysis method)
│   ├── Sub-question 1b (+ analysis method)
│   └── Sub-question 1c (+ analysis method)
├── Hypothesis 2
│   ├── Sub-question 2a (+ analysis method)
│   └── Sub-question 2b (+ analysis method)
└── Hypothesis 3
    ├── Sub-question 3a (+ analysis method)
    └── Sub-question 3b (+ analysis method)
```

Rules:
- MECE at every level
- Same level of abstraction across peers
- Each sub-question has a specific analysis method (internal data, surveys, benchmarking, interviews)
- Relevance matters more than exhaustiveness
- Expect to rebuild this 2-3 times as understanding deepens

### Define the Objective

Write one sentence: What should the audience **learn**, **decide**, or **do** by the end?

For high-stakes work, apply SMART: Specific, Measurable, Action-oriented, Relevant, Time-bound. Also clarify: what is explicitly OUT of scope?

Identify the document type:
- **Live presentation** -- visual, under 20 words per slide, focused on memorability
- **Final deliverable / report** -- comprehensive, readable without presenter
- **Proposal / update doc** -- brief, status-focused, problem + progress + next steps

## Phase 2: Structure the Thinking

### The Pyramid Principle

Three layers, built bottom-up, communicated top-down:

**Bottom: Information** -- raw data points, quotes, research findings

**Middle: Insights** -- synthesized takeaways from groups of information. One sentence per group.

**Top: Main Idea** -- overall takeaway. The "so what." 2-3 sentences you'd tell a CEO in an elevator.

### Label-Sort-Insight (LSI)

The practical workhorse for turning raw information into structured arguments:

**Step 1: Label.** Write a 1-5 word label next to each data point capturing its takeaway. Don't organize yet.

**Step 2: Sort.** Group similar labels using MECE. Find 2-3 distinct themes. Eliminate data points that don't fit.

**Step 3: Insight.** For each group, write ONE sentence capturing what the data points tell you together. One level of abstraction higher than any individual point, but supported by all of them.

### MECE at Every Level

**Mutually Exclusive:** Groups don't overlap.
**Collectively Exhaustive:** Groups together cover everything relevant.

Four uses:
1. **Simplify ideas** -- sort messy lists into distinct groups
2. **Check abstraction levels** -- are all items at the same level?
3. **Test framing** -- if you can't make it MECE, you don't understand the information yet
4. **Find holes** -- what categories should exist but lack data?

Common traps:
- **The "Other" problem** -- if "other" is the biggest category, your top-level groups are too narrow
- **Questions too broad** -- "How could the company improve?" has infinite answers
- **Mixed abstraction levels** -- strategy and tactics can't be peers

### The Rule of Three

- 3 insights supporting the main idea
- Roughly 3 data points per insight
- Aligns with working memory (~4 chunks max)
- 7 ideas? You haven't abstracted enough. 1 idea? You haven't broken it down enough.

### Minto's Three Rules

1. Ideas in each group must be the **same kind of thing**
2. Higher-level ideas must **summarize what's below** -- no new information
3. Ideas must be in **logical order** (chronological, structural, by degree, inductive, or deductive)

### The "So What?" Test

Ask it obsessively. At every level.

- Information: "Ad channel A has 12% ROI, Channel B has 31% ROI"
- So what? -> "Shift 30% of budget from A to B"
- So what? -> "Reallocate $500K this quarter"

If you can't answer "so what?" for a data point, it either doesn't belong or you haven't processed it yet.

### Synthesis vs. Summary

A summary is a condensed restatement. Synthesis is the core implication -- a judgment call.

- Summary: "Our three regions had mixed results"
- Synthesis: "To gain market share, adopt a differentiated pricing policy"

If your titles read like summaries, push harder for the "so what."

### Write a One-Page Memo

Before touching slides, write the argument as prose:
- Paragraph 1: Situation + Complication + Main Recommendation
- Paragraphs 2-4: Each insight with key evidence

Does the argument hold together? Are there holes? Writing forces you to confront logic in a way bullet points never will.

### Build a Ghost Deck

Just slide titles, nothing else. Read them in order. Do they tell a coherent story? Does each lead naturally to the next?

This is your roadmap. Once the ghost deck works, start adding content.

## Phase 3: Build the Communication

### Choosing Your Sequencing Approach

| Approach | When | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct** | Audience agrees; urgent; "just tell me" | Recommendation -> Context -> Arguments + Evidence |
| **Indirect** | Skeptical or hostile audience | Situation + Complication -> Key Ideas -> Recommendation |
| **Concerned** | Everyone agrees on the problem | Complication -> Brief Situation -> Insights -> Recommendation |
| **Aggressive** | High trust; everyone aligned; urgent | Question -> Recommendation |

Direct is the default. Switch to indirect when you sense resistance.

### The Three Principles of Flow

**Vertical Flow:** The title of every slide is the takeaway. Content below proves it. Never use a label as a title. "Q3 Revenue" tells nothing. "Q3 revenue grew 22%, driven by enterprise expansion" tells everything.

Title refinement steps:
1. "The company is improving" -> too vague
2. "The company is growing revenue and shrinking costs" -> still generic
3. "Profitability is increasing" -> cleaner, missing context
4. "Profitability increased for the first time in 5 years due to supply chain investments" -> specific, surprising, actionable

Most people stop at step 1 or 2. Push to step 4.

**Horizontal Flow:** Slide titles, read in sequence, must tell a coherent story. Copy all titles into a document and read them. If they don't make sense without body content, fix the titles or the sequencing.

**Overall Flow:** The deck maps to your pyramid:
1. Title Slide
2. Executive Summary (main recommendation + 3 insights)
3. Section Divider - Insight 1
4. 2-4 slides proving Insight 1
5. Section Divider - Insight 2
6. 2-4 slides proving Insight 2
7. Section Divider - Insight 3
8. 2-4 slides proving Insight 3
9. Recommendation / Next Steps
10. Appendix

### The Ten-Second Test

Show any slide to someone for 10 seconds. Can they tell you:
1. What it's about?
2. Why it matters?
3. What they should think or do?

If any are unclear, rewrite.

### Charts & Data Visualization

Write the message you want the chart to convey before you build it. The chart's only job is to prove the slide title.

Three default chart types (cover 80-90% of needs):
- **Bar charts:** Compare items at a point in time
- **Column charts:** Compare across categories or over time
- **Line charts:** Show trends over time

Avoid pie charts. The human eye is bad at comparing areas and angles. A column chart shows the same data with none of the friction.

Before finalizing any chart:
- Does the chart title match what the chart actually shows?
- Is the most important number the most visually prominent?
- Is there anything that doesn't support the title? (Cut it)
- If the title mentions a concept (e.g., "recession"), is it visually marked on the chart?

Use **illustrative charts** when data is uncertain. Label them "Illustrative" and add footnotes about assumptions. This builds credibility.

### Carmen Simon's Three Modes

#### Perceptive Mode (Ignite the Senses)

Walk the audience through your discovery process, not just conclusions. Use sensory details and timelines: "When I first looked at this data, I was skeptical. An hour later, I was sitting up straight at my desk."

#### Cognitive Mode (Ignite Meaning)

Three levels of communicating any idea:
1. **Concrete facts:** "It costs $2M over 18 months"
2. **Abstract concepts:** "Most ambitious initiative we've undertaken"
3. **Explicit meaning:** "This will save 200 jobs"

Meet your audience where they are. Make abstract terms concrete: "Improved sustainability" communicates nothing. "Cut carbon emissions 40% by 2026" communicates everything.

A picture is memorable when it's easy to label. Text is memorable when it's easy to picture.

#### Affective Mode (Ignite Emotion)

Four emotional levers:
1. **Move toward reward:** "If we do this, we gain X" -> hope, excitement
2. **Move away from reward:** "If we don't act, we miss X" -> urgency
3. **Move away from pain:** "This solution frees us from X" -> relief
4. **Move toward pain:** "We have 90 days before shutdown" -> adrenaline

Ask: After this message, what should the audience *feel*? *think*? *do*?

### Tailoring to Audience Level

| Level | Focus | What They Need |
|---|---|---|
| IC / Manager | Weekly tasks, deliverables | Concrete steps, clear owner, deadline |
| Director / VP | Cross-functional results | Impact, tradeoffs, resource implications |
| C-Suite / Board | Multi-year strategy, competition | So what for the business, competitive context, clear recommendation |

For senior audiences: research how they talk about the business (earnings calls, annual reports). Use their language. Lead with external/competitive context before internal analysis.

### Making It Stick

- Repeat the main message 3 times: introduce, reinforce, summarize
- People remember ~4 chunks max. Main recommendation + 3 insights = best case.
- Plant action triggers: "Next time you're in a quarterly planning meeting, ask this question..."
- Give value in the first 5 minutes
- Make things surprising but familiar

## Critiquing Slides

### Common Patterns to Catch

1. **Title promises growth, chart shows no growth data.** Title and visual must tell the same story.
2. **Seven buzzwords with stock photos.** Remove images that don't directly illustrate the point.
3. **Beautiful slide that says nothing.** Pretty Slide Syndrome. Ask: "What does this tell me to think or do?"
4. **Concept named in title but not marked on chart.** If you mention "recession" or "inflection point," annotate it visually.
5. **Hypothetical data presented as real.** Frame it honestly upfront. Use "illustrative" charts.
6. **Buzzwords masquerading as insight.** "Synergistic value creation" -> "We expect to lose $1.2B this year."

### The EVO Feedback Framework

- **Explanations:** Are there alternate interpretations? Could the audience read this differently?
- **Versions:** Could a different chart type, layout, or framing work better?
- **Opportunities:** What's missing? What insight is buried in the data?

Push toward structural feedback (challenging the argument) not cosmetic feedback (challenging the font).

### Full Quality Checklist

- [ ] Argument written as prose before becoming slides
- [ ] Main message leads the deck, not buried at end
- [ ] 3 key insights (or a compelling reason for different number)
- [ ] Every slide title is a takeaway, not a label
- [ ] 10-second test passed on every slide
- [ ] Titles alone tell a coherent story (outline test)
- [ ] All insights are MECE
- [ ] Chart titles match what charts show
- [ ] No pie charts (unless >50% dominance)
- [ ] No undefined abstract terms
- [ ] Each slide stands alone
- [ ] Emotional register is intentional
- [ ] At least one action trigger planted
- [ ] Key message repeated 3x across the deck
- [ ] Deck tailored to audience's level and language

## Writing Craft

### Word Economy

Cut on sight:

| Kill this | Use this |
|---|---|
| In order to | To |
| Due to the fact that | Because |
| Has the ability to | Can |
| It is important to note that | (delete) |
| At this point in time | Now |
| In the event that | If |

Active verbs always. Contractions always.

### Sentence Craft

**Vary length aggressively.** Short sentences land hard. Then a longer one adds nuance. Three sentences of equal length reads flat.

**Fragments work.** "Not helpful." "This is rare." "Almost no one."

**Always use the serial comma.** "A few dozen people, a handful of offices, and no clear identity." Dropping the conjunction before the last item reads as AI-generated.

### Paragraphs

Short paragraphs dominate. Most are 2-4 sentences. Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis. Never write a paragraph that does two different jobs.

### Tone

**Honest but not harsh.** Critical observations are delivered with understanding of why it happens. "This is a mistake" is always followed by "here's why it happens and here's how to avoid it."

**Confident but not arrogant.** Authority comes from experience and specificity. Hedges when genuine: "in my experience," "I've found." Never as filler.

**No hype.** "Game-changing," "revolutionary," "transformative" don't appear. Neither do exclamation points. The work speaks for itself.

### How to Sound Human in Deliverables

**Be specific, not general.** "Revenue dropped 22% driven by key account losses" not "Revenue declined significantly."

**Use real specifics.** Name the source, the year, the number. "McKinsey's 2024 report found..." not "Industry reports suggest..."

### AI Patterns to Eliminate

**Banned vocabulary.** Never use: delve, realm, embark, landscape (as metaphor), tapestry, multifaceted, leverage (as verb), utilize, crucial, pivotal, paramount, comprehensive, encompasses, cutting-edge, game-changer, unlock (potential), navigate (as metaphor), robust, synergy, streamline, spearhead, foster, elevate, resonate, transformative, underscore, nuanced, poised, seamless, intricacies, groundbreaking, additionally, moreover, furthermore.

**Banned sentence openers.** Never start with: "In today's [X]...", "It's important to note that...", "It's worth noting that...", "This is where [X] comes in...", "When it comes to...", "At the end of the day...", "In the realm of...", "First and foremost...", "In conclusion...", "To summarize..."

**Structural tells.** No perfect parallel structure in every list. No paragraphs that are all the same length. No overly smooth transitions. No opening with a definition. No neat-bow closings. Real thinking is messier -- one point gets two paragraphs, another gets five.

**Significance inflation.** "Marks a pivotal moment," "stands as a testament" -- say what happened.

**Copula avoidance.** Things can just "be" things.

**Negative parallelisms.** "It's not just about X; it's about Y" -- collapse into the direct claim.

**Synonym cycling.** Use the same word when it's the right word. Humans repeat.

**Over-explaining the obvious.** If the audience knows what a meeting is, don't explain it. Trust the reader.

**Transitions that announce themselves.** "Now let's turn to..." On slides, the next slide IS the transition.

**The compliment before the critique.** "While X has many strengths..." Name the problem directly.

**Conclusions that summarize instead of land.** Add one final thought -- a provocation or specific action.

**Cheesy mic-drop endings.** "Do the work. Ship the thing." If the previous paragraph made the point, stop there.

**Staccato enumeration summaries.** "Two axes, four quadrants, a paragraph on each." Write a real sentence or cut it.

**Em dash overuse.** One per slide. Two is a pattern. Three is a tell.

### Slide Text Specifics

**Cards:** Max 3 sentences. Two is better. Lead with the most important word. Bold labels + plain descriptions: "**Rapid prototyping** Idea to MVP in a weekend, not a quarter."

**Subtitles:** Add info the title doesn't contain. Don't restate it. 1-2 sentences max.

**Stat callouts:** Number is the star (60-72pt). Label below reframes, not just names. Comparisons beat absolutes: "10x faster" beats "completed in 2 days."

### Deliverable-Specific Rules

**Slide text:** Max 20 words per slide body for live presentations. Title IS the takeaway. Bold labels on bullet points.

**Memos:** Lead with recommendation in paragraph 1. One insight per section. Section headers are insight statements.

**Emails:** Subject line = main idea. First sentence = the ask. Body = numbered insights. Close = deadline.

**Exec summaries:** Main recommendation + 3 insights with one line of evidence each. One page max.

### Final Writing Check

1. Flag every banned vocabulary word. Replace or cut.
2. Check for three consecutive same-length sentences. Break the pattern.
3. Find empty hype. Replace with numbers.
4. Scan for banned sentence openers. Delete.
5. Check for structural tells -- all paragraphs same length? All lists parallel?
6. Read it out loud. Press release or smart colleague?
7. The specificity test: could a language model have generated this without real-world knowledge?
